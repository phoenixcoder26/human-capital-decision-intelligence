Statistical Findings
533 eligible employees were selected from the 1,000-employee synthetic workforce dataset using flight risk, retention priority, career stall, and engagement criteria.
266 treatment / 267 control — groups were statistically balanced on all 14 pre-intervention characteristics, confirming valid randomisation.
The enhanced intervention improved engagement by +4.6 points more than control — a statistically significant effect (p < 0.001) with a medium Cohen's d effect size.
Flight risk decreased in the treatment group post-intervention, with the reduction strongest among high-performing employees with high baseline risk scores.
Business Interpretation
What the Results Mean for HR
Short-term attrition reduction was small and not statistically significant — this is expected at 3 months, as engagement improvements typically precede attrition changes by 6–12 months.
High-performing flight risks and career-stalled employees responded most strongly — suggesting the intervention should be prioritised for these two segments rather than applied uniformly.
The engagement improvement is a leading indicator — HR leaders should monitor the treatment cohort for 6–12 months to capture the full attrition impact before scaling the programme.
Next steps: Scale to the full eligible population with segment-priority ordering, run a second randomised cohort to confirm external validity, and integrate findings into the causal inference module.
